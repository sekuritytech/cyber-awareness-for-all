Module 2 of 8 · Personal Information

🔒 Protecting Your Personal Info

Your information is like treasure. Once you give it away, you can't get it back. Learn what's safe to share — and what's not.

🚫

Never Share Online

Full name, home address, school, phone number, or daily routine with anyone you haven't met in real life.

📸

Photos Reveal More Than You Think

School uniforms, street signs, and location data embedded in photos can reveal exactly where you live and study.

🎭

Always Use a Username

Use a fun, neutral nickname online. Never include your real name, birth year, or location in your username.

👆 Click each scenario to reveal what's happening:

DangerThe Profile Setup Mistake
Priya creates a new TikTok profile. She uses her full name, adds her school name in the bio, and posts a photo in her school uniform near a recognisable street sign.
Click to see what's wrong ↓
⚠️ This reveals too much. Her full name, school, uniform, and location together make it easy for a stranger to find her in real life. A safe profile uses a nickname, no school name, and no identifying photos. She should also set her account to private.
SafeThe Smart Gamer
Arjun joins an online game and creates a profile with the username "StarBlaster99", uses a cartoon avatar, leaves the bio blank, and doesn't mention his age, city, or school anywhere.
Click to see why this is good ↓
✅ This is a safe online profile. No real name, no photo, no location. Other players can only see what Arjun wants them to see. He can enjoy the game without putting himself at risk.
WarningThe "Check-In" Post
Meera posts: "SO excited — Mum and Dad away till Sunday, home alone watching Netflix 🏠❤️" with a location tag of her city and neighbourhood.
Click to understand the risk ↓
⚠️ This post reveals a dangerous combination: she's home alone, her parents are away, and her location is tagged. Even if her account is set to "friends only," this type of post creates real safety risks. Never share that you're home alone online.

❌ Not quite. Personal details like your real name, age, location, and school should never appear on a public gaming profile. A safe profile uses only a neutral username and avatar.

Module 3 of 8 · Phishing & Scams

🎣 Recognising Phishing & Scams

Scammers are experts at making fake messages look real. Learn the telltale signs — before you click.

1

Urgency is a red flag

"Act NOW or your account will be deleted!" — Real companies never pressure you like this. Urgency is designed to stop you thinking clearly.

2

Too good to be true = almost certainly false

"You've won 10,000 Robux!" "Free PS5!" If you didn't enter a competition, you didn't win. These links lead to malware or stolen credentials.

3

Check the sender's real address

Phishing emails often use addresses like [email protected] or [email protected] (note the "1" not "l"). Always check carefully.

4

Never enter passwords on a link you clicked

Type website addresses directly into your browser bar. A link in a message might take you to a look-alike fake site designed to steal your login.

PhishingThe "Verify Your Account" Email
You receive an email: "Dear User, your Minecraft account has been flagged for suspicious activity. Click here to verify your account within 24 hours or it will be permanently deleted." The sender is: [email protected]
Click to identify this threat ↓
🎣 Classic phishing. Signs: (1) Urgency — "24 hours or deleted" (2) Fear tactic — "suspicious activity" (3) Fake sender domain — real Minecraft support is @mojang.com, not "minecraftsupport-verify.com". Delete this email. Never click the link. Report it to a parent.
ScamThe Free V-Bucks Generator
A YouTube comment says: "I got FREE V-Bucks from this site! 🔥 Just enter your username and password to claim yours: [link]" It has 847 likes and lots of comments saying "Thanks, it worked!"
Click to see the truth ↓
⚠️ This is a credential-stealing scam. Free V-Bucks generators do not exist. The "likes" and "it worked" comments are fake — bots or paid accounts. Entering your username and password gives scammers full access to your Fortnite (and possibly other) accounts. Never enter game credentials on third-party sites.
SafeThe Real Password Reset
You forgot your Instagram password and clicked "Forgot password?" Instagram sends an email from [email protected] with a reset link. The link goes to instagram.com.
Click to confirm why this is safe ↓
✅ This is a legitimate reset email. Key checks: (1) You initiated it — you clicked "forgot password" (2) Sender is @mail.instagram.com — the real domain (3) Link goes to instagram.com, not a look-alike. Always verify the URL bar after clicking.
Quiz: You get a WhatsApp message from an unknown number: "You've won a brand new iPhone 15! Tap here to claim your prize NOW before it expires!" What should you do?
You didn't enter any competition. The message has a short link you don't recognise.
A
Tap the link quickly before the prize expires
B
Forward it to friends so they can claim a prize too
C
Delete the message and block the number — it's a scam
D
Reply asking for more details about the prize
✅ Correct! You didn't enter a competition, so you can't have won. The link is phishing — designed to steal your data or infect your device. Delete and block. Never reply or forward scam messages.
❌ That's what scammers want you to do! Clicking, forwarding, or replying all confirm your number is active and can spread the scam. Always delete unexpected "prize" messages. You cannot win something you didn't enter.
Module 4 of 8 · Online Grooming

😈 Understanding Online Grooming

Grooming happens gradually. Knowing the warning signs helps you recognise it early — and stay safe.

🔍

What Is Grooming?

When an adult builds a fake friendship with a child online to gain their trust — often with the intention of exploitation or abuse.

🕐

It Happens Slowly

Groomers are patient. They spend weeks or months building "friendship" before making requests or suggestions that cross the line.

🤫

Secrets Are the #1 Warning Sign

If someone tells you to keep your conversations secret from parents — that is a serious red flag, regardless of who they claim to be.

⚠️

"Keep this between us" — Always a red flag

Trusted adults and real friends never ask children to hide conversations from parents or guardians.

⚠️

Unexpected gifts, game credits, or money

Gifts create a sense of obligation. If an online "friend" sends gifts without being asked, ask yourself why.

⚠️

Requests for photos or video calls

A trusted online acquaintance does not need photos of you. Any request for personal images should be reported to a parent immediately.

⚠️

Moving to a private platform quickly

"Let's talk on WhatsApp instead" — moving away from moderated platforms removes protective oversight.

🛑

STOP

Stop all communication immediately. Don't explain yourself — just stop responding.

📸

SCREENSHOT

Take screenshots of the conversation as evidence before blocking.

🚫

BLOCK & REPORT

Block the account and use the platform's report function.

🗣️

TELL A TRUSTED ADULT

Tell a parent, teacher, or guardian immediately. You will not get in trouble — you did nothing wrong.

🆘

Need help now? Childline India: 1098 (free, 24/7) · Childline UK: 0800 1111 · NCMEC: 1-800-843-5678 · India Cybercrime: cybercrime.gov.in

Quiz: Someone you met in an online game says they are 14, sends you gifts and is very friendly. After a few weeks they ask for a photo of you and say "don't tell your parents." What should you do?
This person feels like a friend. But something feels uncomfortable about this request.
A
Send a photo — you've known them for weeks and they seem nice
B
Ask them why they want it before deciding
C
Stop talking, screenshot the chat, and tell a parent immediately
D
Block them but don't tell anyone — it's embarrassing
✅ Correct! "Don't tell your parents" is the clearest grooming warning sign there is. You can never verify who someone really is online. Screenshot → Block → Tell a trusted adult. You did nothing wrong.
❌ "Don't tell your parents" is an immediate red flag — regardless of how long you've known someone online. A real friend never asks for secrecy from your parents. Stop, screenshot, block, and tell a trusted adult right away.
Module 5 of 8 · Cyberbullying

💬 Cyberbullying — Recognise, Respond, Report

Online words cause real pain. Learn how to identify cyberbullying, protect yourself, and support others.

✅ Positive Online Behaviour
Giving genuine compliments and encouragement
Sharing content with permission of the person in it
Respectfully disagreeing in comments
Supporting friends who are struggling online
Reporting harmful content instead of sharing it
❌ Cyberbullying Behaviours
Sending mean, threatening, or insulting messages
Sharing embarrassing photos without consent
Creating fake profiles to mock someone
Deliberately excluding someone from group chats
Spreading rumours or lies about someone online
CyberbullyingThe Humiliating Post
Someone at school screenshots a funny photo of you from a private group chat and posts it publicly on Instagram, tagging your name and writing a mocking caption. It gets 50 comments laughing at you.
Click to see what to do ↓
⚠️ This is cyberbullying — and possibly illegal. Step 1: Screenshot the post and all comments as evidence. Step 2: Report the post to Instagram for "bullying or harassment." Step 3: Tell a trusted adult — parent or teacher. Step 4: Ask the school for support. Do NOT retaliate — it can make things worse legally.
Bystander MomentThe Group Chat Pile-On
In a class WhatsApp group, several people start making fun of a classmate. Others are laughing along or sending laugh emojis. You're not saying anything, but you're still in the group.
Click to understand your role ↓
💡 Silence can feel like approval. As a bystander, you have power too. Options: (1) Privately message the person being targeted to show support. (2) Say in the group "This isn't funny, let's stop." (3) Screenshot and report to a trusted adult. Staying silent and hoping it stops rarely works — and can make the target feel more alone.
Quiz: Your classmate posts a humiliating photo of you online without your permission. You feel hurt and angry. What is the BEST first step?
The photo is getting likes and mean comments. Your instinct is to react immediately.
A
Post something equally humiliating about them in revenge
B
Ignore it completely — it will probably blow over
C
Screenshot everything, then tell a trusted adult and report the post
D
Get all your friends to report it without telling an adult
✅ Correct! Screenshot first (evidence disappears when you block). Then tell a trusted adult — they can help you report to the school and platform officially. You don't have to handle this alone.
❌ Retaliation escalates the situation and can get you in trouble too. Ignoring gives it more time to spread. Always: Screenshot → Tell an adult → Report officially. These three steps protect you most effectively.
Module 6 of 8 · AI & Deepfakes

🤖 AI, Deepfakes & Misinformation

AI can now create realistic fake videos, voices, and photos of real people. Learn to spot them — and protect yourself.

🎭

What Is a Deepfake?

An AI-generated fake video or image that replaces someone's face or voice with another person's — often without their knowledge or consent.

📞

Voice Cloning

AI can clone anyone's voice from just a few seconds of audio. Scammers use this to impersonate family members in fake "emergency" calls.

🔍

How to Spot Fakes

Look for: unnatural blinking, blurry edges around the face, inconsistent lighting, audio that doesn't quite match mouth movement.

AI ScamThe Fake Emergency Call
You receive a frantic call that sounds exactly like your mum: "I've been in an accident and I need you to send ₹5,000 to this number right away. Don't tell Dad. Please hurry." The voice sounds completely real.
Click to understand this threat ↓
🤖 This is an AI voice cloning scam. Scammers can clone voices from social media videos. Always hang up and call the person back directly on their known number to verify. Never transfer money based on an unexpected call, even if the voice sounds familiar. Create a family "safe word" for real emergencies.
DeepfakeThe Viral School Video
A video goes around your school showing what appears to be a classmate saying something offensive. Everyone is sharing it and getting angry. But when you look closely, the face movements look slightly "off."
Click to see what to do ↓
💡 Pause before reacting. This could be a deepfake designed to damage someone's reputation. Signs of a deepfake: unnatural blinking, face edges look blurry, audio slightly out of sync. Don't share until verified. Report to a trusted adult. The person in the video may be an innocent victim of AI manipulation.
Quiz: A shocking video clip goes viral showing a famous person saying something terrible. It has millions of views. What should you do before sharing it?
The content is outrageous. Your first instinct is to share it with everyone you know.
A
Share it immediately — millions of people can't all be wrong
B
Pause, check a trusted news source, and look for signs of manipulation
C
Share it but add "I'm not sure this is real" in your caption
D
Ask your friends what they think, then share if they agree
✅ Correct! Viral reach doesn't equal truth — deepfakes spread fastest because they trigger outrage. Always check a second trusted source (BBC, Reuters, AP) before sharing anything shocking. Sharing a deepfake — even innocently — causes real harm to real people.
❌ Popularity is not the same as truth. Deepfakes are designed to go viral. Before sharing anything outrageous, verify it with a trusted news source. If it turns out to be fake, you've helped spread misinformation — and caused harm to an innocent person.
Module 7 of 8 · Online Gaming Safety

🎮 Staying Safe While Gaming Online

Online games are where millions of children socialise, play, and learn — and where predators increasingly look for targets.

✅ Safe Gaming Habits
Use a neutral username with no real name or age
Play with friends you know in real life
Keep all chat to game-related topics only
Report offensive players using in-game tools
Get parent approval before in-app purchases
Know how to mute, block, and report
❌ Risky Gaming Habits
Sharing personal info in voice/text chat
Accepting gifts or credits from strangers
Clicking external links from other players
Moving to private chat outside the game
Agreeing to meet a "gaming friend" in person
Giving your account to someone to "level up"
Gaming DangerThe Too-Generous Stranger
While playing an online game, a player you've never met sends you expensive in-game items and says "I just want to be friends and help you level up. Can we move to Discord to chat more easily?"
Click to see the red flags ↓
⚠️ Multiple grooming red flags here. (1) Unsolicited expensive gifts create a sense of obligation. (2) Wanting to move to a private, unmoderated platform (Discord) removes protective oversight. Block this player, report them in-game, and tell a parent about the conversation. Do not move the conversation off the platform.
Scam AttemptAccount Recovery Scam
"Hi! I'm a Roblox support agent. We've detected suspicious activity on your account. Give me your username and password so I can secure it for you."
Click to understand this scam ↓
⚠️ Roblox, Fortnite, or any real game company will NEVER ask for your password. This is account theft. Real support always goes through the official website's help centre — never through in-game chat. Ignore, report the player, and change your password if you felt pressured.
Quiz: A player you've only ever met online says they'll be at a shopping mall in your city this weekend and wants to meet up in person. They seem really nice. What should you do?
A
Meet them — you've played together for months, they're safe
B
Go but bring a friend so it's safer
C
Never meet — tell a trusted adult about the request immediately
D
Ask for a video call first to confirm they are who they say
✅ Correct! You cannot verify who someone really is online — even after months of playing together. Meeting requests from online-only friends are a critical warning sign. Always tell a trusted adult. A real friend who cares for your safety would understand.
❌ Online-only relationships cannot be fully verified, no matter how long they've lasted. Meeting someone from online in person — without a parent's knowledge and involvement — is one of the most dangerous situations children can face. Always refuse and immediately tell a trusted adult.
